Angle with its Bisector

You can measure angles, contruct polygons, and perform transformations on objects in GeoGebra. In this example, a parallelogram was constructed by first adding the line segment AB, then rotating B around A and an arbitrary other point C by the same angle. When the distance between that C and the intersection of the lines connecting A and C with their respective rotations of B is equal to AB.

In GeoGebra, you can draw polygons or lines and then add points onto the perimeter or line. Even if you move other elements or change the size of line segments, those points will stay on the line or curve they were constructed on. In this example, A is the center of the circle, and B and C are points on the perimeter of the circle. Even if we changed the size of the circle or moved B or C around, both points would remain on the perimeter of the circle.

There are some things you do so often in geometry that GeoGebra gives you tools that make it easier. One of those tools is the angle bisector tool. In this example, we used the angle bisector tool on angle ABC to find its bisector, which is AD. If we changed the size of angle ABC, the point D would move as well so that AD is always the bisector of ABC. This and other tools are very useful when making contructions in GeoGebra.
